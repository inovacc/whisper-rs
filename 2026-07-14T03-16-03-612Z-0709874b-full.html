Beyond Speech-To-Text — Towards more robust and informative ASR by increasing transcription readability

Teo Yoke Sheng Clifton
11 min read
·
Mar 16, 2026

Hello! I am Clifton, from the Language AI R&D Team within the Home Team Science and Technology Agency (HTX), and today I will be writing about my work here in HTX, mostly focusing on Speech AI and research contributing to more robust Automatic Speech recognition (ASR)! ASR technology is increasingly rampant, chances are if you watch Youtube, you would have seen automatic captions being generated — a feature that has been in place since 2009 that drastically improved following the AI wave.

Though ASR has essentially plateaued in performance (Word Error Rate or WER) for high resource languages (extensible to lower resource languages/accents via finetuning given enough data — refer to our medium article here to see how we did so for the Singaporean context), there are still many areas of speech research that can push ASR to the next level:

  • Text can be aligned in time (i.e. CTC/forced alignment) to obtain timestamp for subtitles or to search audio for words of interest
  • Speech recognition can be done for multiple languages at once (multilingual code switching)
  • Speaker information can be extracted to determine who spoke what when (i.e. diarisation)
  • Prosodic information can be analysed to examine emotive states of speakers
  • Sources in an audio stream can be separated
  • And so on…

With so many possible topics to explore in the ASR realm, it may be quite difficult to determine the next steps, and what to research into — every topic can provide tangible improvements to pipelines leveraging ASR. In the end, we chose to do improvements to the pipeline incrementally, building up important foundations via better alignment of transcription (e.g. digit to spoken numbers), software and hardware diarisation, speaker enrolment, and constant incremental improvements to our finetuning pipeline. These areas of work would strengthen the foundations of our team’s pipeline by providing better readability to ASR transcriptions.

What to improve in ASR

Our existing Whisper-based framework was already performing ASR for Singaporean speech with diarisation, that is closely mimicking the WhisperX pipeline (i.e. Whisper — Wav2Vec2 Forced Alignment — Pyannote Speaker Diarisation) and yielding decent performance. How else do we then improve our ASR pipeline? We started by exposing some of the problems that we faced when building up our ASR pipeline:

Problem 1 — Word Error Rate (WER) does not give the full picture

Finetuning Whisper on IMDA NSC part 3 did give transcription a boost in accuracy performance (or a drop in WER) for Singaporean English. However, Whisper’s original pre-training corpus contains a large amount of web data, which contributed to hallucinated transcription on learned patterns that may not correspond to actual audio. Consequently, even though WER has dropped in the large part, when it matters, hallucinated output not only inflates WER but also result in reduction in readability and reliability of transcripts. A few examples of harmful hallucinations are given in the table below.

Problem 2 — Written forms of numbers does not align well with speech

When spoken, numbers differ greatly from their written representation. Take “5556 — Five thousand five hundred and fifty six” for example; generating it via ASR may lead to “5000 500 and 56”. This creates two sub problems for us:

  1. Obtaining numerical digit output requires careful consideration — The boundaries of speech for each utterance of a number cannot be clearly defined. (For example, for Five thousand five hundred and fifty six, “5556” and “5500, and 56” could both be valid utterances within a sentence.)
  2. Forced alignment fails when digits are generated — Timestamp information of digits cannot be reliably obtained as forced alignment expects zero ambiguity in speech and transcription pairs, whereas one written form of a number has multiple spoken forms. “5556” can be interpreted as any of the following:

five thousand five hundred and fifty six

five thousand five hundred fifty six

five five five six

Thus, some form of processing is required for alignment to work, and to obtain the desired output (in this case, digits only).

Problem 3 — Online speaker diarisation struggles with speech in Singaporean meetings

Online speaker diarisation refers to the case of multiple speaker speech being streamed in real time, and the attempt to label who spoke what, when. This is challenging as Singaporeans typically speak with frequent interruptions and a fast pace of speech. This phenomenon is illustrated in the timestamped transcript below.

00:00:01.354 -> 00:00:03.547 <Speaker 1>: Another speaker speaks quickly after me

00:00:03.625 -> 00:00:06.142 <Speaker 2>: Which is encountered often during discussi — [Interuption/overlap] - ons

00:00:06.052 -> 00:00:08.651 <Speaker 1>: With occasional interruptions as well

This makes online speaker diarisation difficult to do with high accuracy as speaker segments belonging to different speakers will not have a well-defined border.

Enhancing ASR via Research and Engineering

Engineering hallucinations away

When our existing research is not yet able to reliably solve issues (such as hallucination), our AI-Engineering team (led by

Chin Zhuo Sheng
) harnesses raw engineering prowess instead. The team came up with the idea of comparing Whisper’s transcription against Wav2Vec2’s during streaming inference, which is much more reliable due to the differences in objectives of the two models:

  • Wav2Vec2 being more focused on outputting phone labels and using CTC loss to align these labels to words.
  • Whisper looking across a 30 seconds chunk of audio to output speech features for its decoder to reference, in addition to previous text tokens, to autoregressively generate text tokens. The flawed data during pre-training + decoding process is likely what causes the hallucination problem

By flagging severe differences in transcription, hallucination can be “caught” and removed.

Enhancing readability by eliminating written numbers in transcription

Finetuning whisper to transcribe Singaporean speech well, unfortunately for us, made generated transcripts more likely to output written numbers, due to the formatting of the transcripts that we use as our training data. We explored a few solutions:

  1. The quickest and easiest way to eliminate this was by suppressing numerals.
  2. We also explored prompting whisper to nudge it to transcribe in alphabetical spoken form as well, which helped further reduce written number output.
  3. We also post processed the transcript to convert alphabetical spoken forms to formats that are more easily readable to align better with what our users want.

These “fixes” can be found in the code chunk below:

import whisperx

whisper_prompt = "The following audio contains expressions of quantity and measurement
that should be represented as spelled-out words rather than symbols.
Ensure all original marks of punctuation and indicators of emphasis,
such as capital letters, are faithfully maintained throughout the
transcription. For instance, a quantity of items should appear as
'ten units' not 'one zero units'. A monetary value should be 'twenty dollars'
instead of 'two zero dollars'. A chronological indication should be
'nineteen ninety-nine' instead of 'one nine nine nine'. Preserve specific
formatting like 'P.O. Box' or similar constructs. The aim is for completely
textual representations of all numerical concepts."

whisperx.load_model(
        "large-v2",
        device,
        compute_type=compute_type,
        language=language,
        asr_options={
            "beam_size": 5,
            "suppress_numerals": True,
            "initial_prompt": whisper_prompt,
            "length_penalty": 1.0,
        },
    )

This is of course not the only, or “best” solution to this problem as others have also explored using LLMs or end-to-end models to post process the transcription instead.

Diarisation (Software)

Before we discuss more on the solutions we explored for online speaker diarisation (or streaming/real time diarisation), let’s go through a very quick crash course on diarisation. To summarise, diarisation is simply the task of determining who spoke what, when. Diarisation typically refers to software diarisation, whereby an audio clip is passed through a pipeline which:

1) Detects speech segment and the timestamps via Voice Activity Detection (VAD)

2) Determines if there are speaker switches within these segments via Speaker Segmentation

3) Clusters speech segment embeddings based on some form of similarity metric (eg. cosine similarity)

The metric we use to evaluate diarisation performance is the Diarisation Error Rate (DER):

DER is calculated by adding up errors arising from the following:

  • False Alarm — Non speech segments incorrectly assigned to a speaker in green
  • Missed Detection — Speech segments spoken by a speaker, but is not assigned to any speakers
  • Speaker Confusion — Speech segments incorrectly assigned to the wrong speaker

and dividing by the total length of speech. The key thing to take note here is that two out of three components of DER (False Alarm and Missed Detection) are highly dependent on whether voice activity is detected properly. Thus, the key component that we chose to focus on is Speaker Confusion, as it affects user friendliness of transcription by the margin. Just imagine reading an interview excerpt that consistently labels the interviewer as the interviewee and vice versa, where labeling either speakers for each speech segments would still make sense:

Interviewer (correctly labelled): What about the weather? Singapore‘s weather has been very volatile lately.

Interviewee (wrongly labelled): I was actually going to try cycling to work but it started to pour, so I scrapped that thought.

Interviewer (wrongly labelled) : Oh yes, this January’s rainfall is actually a record high for singapore!

That being said, we cannot simply forgo the other two components and solely focus on Speaker Confusion. For example, Speaker Confusion can effectively be zero when Missed Detection is 100%, which may happen in cases like overly noisy audio with loud background noises, which makes it hard for VAD to be reliable.

Some popular open sourced diarisation frameworks available today include the use of Silero VAD for streaming voice activity detection, and Pyannote for the segmentation and embedding models. In our testing, diarisation performance is generally decent across multiple frameworks for batch diarisation, but performance is lacking in live settings.

Issues faced during live diarisation (and transcription)

Live transcription and diarisation has two key differences from batch:

  1. Batch processing allows both transcription and diarisation models to observe (up to) the full audio length, which allows for better modelling of speaker features, as well as improved segmentation and clustering performance
  2. To retain the speed of live transcription, speaker embeddings would need to be formed at the start to provide a point of comparison to newly embedded speech, which can either be from new speakers or previously encountered speakers. These embeddings are referred to as speaker attractors. Misclassifications (that may arise from a segment containing overlapping speech, or successive speaker speech) during inference will contribute to performance degradations due to erroneous updates to these speaker attractors, leading to even more misclassifications.

Several frameworks then aim to improve live diarisation performance:

Diart

Diart is a diarisation framework that does not assume an upper bound to the number of speakers, directly outputting multi-speaker activity within a rolling audio buffer, which is compared against (constantly updated) speaker embeddings of speakers encountered thus far. There is an initial latency of 5 seconds to initialize global speaker embeddings before it is used to compare against the rolling buffer of locally segmented speech embeddings, which will then be clustered and compared against the global speaker embeddings.

Sortformer v2

Sortformer introduces sort loss, where speakers are indexed based on arrival timing, such that prediction will always align with this ordering. It also introduces an Arrival Order Speaker Cache (AOSC) which stores speaker embeddings in order of arrival, with new speakers being appended as a new index. Speech from previous speakers will be appended to their earlier segments in their respective index within the AOSC.

However, from real-world testing, live diarisation still only performs well when there are clearly defined pauses in between speaker speech since misclassifications during inference deteriorates performance relatively quickly as speaker attractors will be updated with these errors as well.

Solving the speaker attractor problem

A quick solution to bypass badly modelled speaker attractors would be to collect clean speech from speakers before a meeting, using them as the ground truth embeddings for computing similarities (commonly cosine similarity) between newly detected speech within a meeting recording against all the embeddings in a process otherwise known as speaker identification. The speaker label is then returned:

The key disadvantage via this approach is the need for meeting attendees to record clean audio that is ideally phonetically balanced (see Harvard Sentences for an example), and similar to their conversational style for reference speech embeddings to be accurate during inference.

Other approaches to diarisation (Hardware) to explore

Diarisation (Software) may be a difficult task, but having multiple channels of audio simplifies the problem slightly by providing us with spatial information to work with. Spatial information arises from the way sound is propagated — sound waves travel at approximately 343m/s, meaning that any given microphone pair in a multi-channel recording set up will pick up the sound at different timings, frequency spectrums, and amplitudes. With these information at hand, Direction Of Arrival (DOA) can then be estimated. The following approaches are some of the methods that can estimate DOA:

Subspace based

These methods aim to separate audio signals into signal (information of the sources) and noise subspaces (in ideal cases, containing no source information), and through the assumption of source signals being uncorrelated and number of sensors >= number of sources, attempt to determine the DOA.

Time Delay Of Arrival

These methods attempt to estimate difference in time needed for source(s) to travel to each microphones assuming that there is one dominant path it travels by. The estimation is done by finding peaks where signals match at the estimated time delay

Mic1’s signal @ time 1 ≈ Mic2’s signal @ (time 1 + delay)

With more than two microphones, using the estimated delays between all microphone pairs can then point us in an approximate DOA. With only two microphones, sources located equidistant from the two microphones, can be located on either side perpendicular to the center of the microphone pair. But with just one more microphone added, this eliminates this ambiguity. The DOA is calculated using distance between microphone pairs (d), speed of sound (c), time delay (t):

Neural Networks

Given enough similar data to actual deployment set-ups, and if training data does not differ from what will be recorded during meetings, Neural Networks can do very well for DOA estimation. It can inherently circumvent problems phased (pun intended) by other methods, like overlapping speech, reverberation, meaningless but constant background noise by learning directly from training data how to deal with these without needing additional specialized components such as speech enhancement and overlapped speech detection. However, the assumption of training data closely matching (Distance of source from microphone array, background noise volume and type, speaker locations relative to array, etc.) meeting recordings must be met, otherwise performance degrades much faster than other methods.

These are just a few topics to explore further. There are many other ways that diarisation can be achieved (Audio-Visual speaker diarisation, isolating speaker audio streams etc.) — still, these do provide a wide enough base for us to dive deeper into strengthening our diarisation capabilities!

Concluding thoughts

ASR already provides a big quality of life improvement, with many direct applications. There are however many components of an ASR pipeline to improve upon, to truly push the usefulness of ASR systems to the next level, as with all AI systems. Do subscribe to our medium page and anticipate the many other topics we will explore in the near future — these may well span beyond building robust ASR pipelines!
